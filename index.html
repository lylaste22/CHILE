CHILE 2015

Chile-Harvard Innovative Learning Exchange 2015

About

CHILE 2015 is a joint research effort between the Institute for Applied Computational Science (IACS) at Harvard University and the Center for Mathematical Modeling (CMM) at the Universidad de Chile. From Jan 4 to Jan 17, 2015, students from each university worked in three teams to address real-world computational challenges drawn from a cutting-edge astronomical endeavor, the Dark Energy Camera.

Introduction

The Dark Energy Survey (DES) is an optical/near-infrared survey that aims to probe the dynamics of the expansion of the universe. The survey uses the 4-meter Victor M. Blanco Telescope located at Cerro Tololo Inter-American Observatory in Chile, outfitted with a Dark Energy Camera (DECam). This camera allows for more sensitive images in the red part of the visible spectrum and in the near infrared, in comparison to previous instruments.

The Blanco telescope was used in the 90s to measure the expansion of the universe and its deceleration. Astronomers subsequently observed that the rate of expansion was in fact increasing. According to Einstein's theory of General Relativity however, gravity should lead to a slowing of the expansion. Two new premises are therefore possible: either 75% of the universe exists in an exotic form, now called dark energy, that exhibits a gravitational force opposite to the attractive gravity of ordinary matter accounting for the observed acceleration or General Relativity must be replaced by a new theory of gravity on cosmic scales. DECam is being used to probe the origin of the accelerating universe.

This camera contains 74 CCD’s with 570 Megapixels each and 400 images are collected per night amounting to approximately 1 TByte/night.

Data

The data for this project included CCD images from the Victor M. Blanco Telescope’s Dark Energy Camera (DECam) at the Cerro Tololo Inter-American Observatory.

  • forty fields observed, during five nights, four times per nights, whith g filter, every image covers 3 sq. deg.
  • aprox 2 TBytes of data in FITS format.
